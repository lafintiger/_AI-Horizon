üéØ Research Objective & Task-Based Analysis

Primary Research Goal: Analyze how artificial intelligence is transforming specific cybersecurity tasks, not just job roles. This research focuses on the granular, day-to-day activities that cybersecurity professionals perform.

Key Distinction: Tasks vs Jobs

This research prioritizes tasks over job titles because:

  • Tasks are actionable: Specific activities that can be measured and analyzed
  • Tasks evolve faster: While job roles change slowly, tasks transform rapidly with AI adoption
  • Tasks are transferable: Skills and activities that apply across multiple roles
  • Tasks align with DCWF: The DoD framework is task-centric, not role-centric

üìã DoD Cybersecurity Workforce Framework (DCWF) Integration

This analysis is grounded in the DoD Cybersecurity Workforce Framework (DCWF), which provides a standardized taxonomy of cybersecurity tasks, knowledge, skills, and abilities.

Why DCWF?

  • Comprehensive task catalog covering all cybersecurity domains
  • Standard DoD framework for workforce development
  • Task-based structure aligns with our research methodology
  • Regular updates reflect evolving cybersecurity landscape

Framework Access: View DCWF Documentation ‚Üí

Reference: Department of Defense. (2024). DoD Cybersecurity Workforce Framework (DCWF). Retrieved from https://public.cyber.mil/wid/dcwf/

1 üéØ Key Assumptions

Our analysis is built on the following foundational assumptions about AI's impact on cybersecurity workforce:

  • Categorical Framework: AI impact on cybersecurity roles can be meaningfully categorized into four distinct types: Replace, Augment, New Tasks, and Human-Only tasks.
  • Timeframe Relevance: Current discussions (2024-2025) provide meaningful insights into near-term (1-3 year) workforce transformations.
  • Source Authority: Authoritative sources (Gartner, Forrester, SANS, academic institutions) provide more reliable predictions than general news sources.
  • Indicator Validity: Specific terminology and phrases can reliably indicate the type of AI impact being discussed.
  • Perplexity Aggregation: Perplexity AI provides quality aggregated insights from multiple sources, though individual source attribution is limited.
  • Content Volume Correlation: Longer, more detailed content generally indicates more comprehensive analysis and higher quality insights.
  • Indicator Frequency: The frequency of key indicators within content correlates with the strength of that category's relevance.
‚ö†Ô∏è Current Limitations:
  • Small sample size (1 article per category in current test)
  • Single data source (Perplexity) may introduce bias
  • No longitudinal trend analysis yet
  • Limited validation of indicator effectiveness

4 üìä Scoring & Weighting Methodology

üéØ Targeting Effectiveness Score

Formula: Targeting_Effectiveness = (Total_Indicators_Found / Max_Possible_Indicators) √ó 100 Where: ‚Ä¢ Total_Indicators_Found = Sum of all indicator matches across all articles in category ‚Ä¢ Max_Possible_Indicators = Number_of_Indicators √ó Number_of_Articles ‚Ä¢ Result: Percentage (0-100%) indicating search query effectiveness Academic Foundation: Based on information retrieval precision metrics (Manning et al., 2008) and TF-IDF weighting schemes (Salton & Buckley, 1988) for term importance assessment.

üèõÔ∏è Source Credibility Assessment

Multi-Factor Credibility Score: Credibility = 0.4 √ó Source_Authority + 0.3 √ó Domain_Expertise + 0.2 √ó Publication_Recency + 0.1 √ó Citation_Count Component Definitions: ‚Ä¢ Source_Authority: Domain authority based on institutional credibility (Hirsch, 2005) ‚Ä¢ Domain_Expertise: Cybersecurity field relevance (0.0-1.0 scale) ‚Ä¢ Publication_Recency: Temporal relevance with exponential decay weighting ‚Ä¢ Citation_Count: Academic/professional citation metrics where available Recognized Authority Domains: ‚Ä¢ gartner.com, forrester.com, mckinsey.com (Industry analysis: 0.9-1.0) ‚Ä¢ sans.org, nist.gov, cisa.gov (Government/standards: 0.8-0.9) ‚Ä¢ ieee.org, acm.org, arxiv.org (Academic: 0.7-0.9) ‚Ä¢ Vendor technical blogs: 0.5-0.7 (context dependent) Academic Foundation: Weights derived from source reliability research (Castillo et al., 2011) and authority assessment methodologies adapted for cybersecurity domain expertise.

üìè Content Quality Metrics

Content Quality Score: Quality = 0.3 √ó Length_Score + 0.2 √ó Keyword_Density + 0.2 √ó Readability + 0.3 √ó Structure_Score Component Calculations: ‚Ä¢ Length_Score: Min-max normalized article length (Han et al., 2011) ‚Ä¢ Keyword_Density: TF-IDF weighted density of domain terms (Salton & Buckley, 1988) ‚Ä¢ Readability: Flesch-Kincaid readability assessment (Flesch et al., 1975) ‚Ä¢ Structure_Score: Content organization and formatting quality Indicator Density Formula: Density = (Total_Indicators_Found / Total_Character_Count) √ó 1000 (Indicators per 1000 characters) Academic Foundation: Combines readability research (Flesch-Kincaid), information theory (Shannon, 1948), and weighted scoring principles (Saaty, 1980) for multi-criteria assessment.

üî¨ Semantic Similarity Assessment

Semantic Similarity Score: Similarity = cosine_similarity(SBERT_embedding(query), SBERT_embedding(content)) Implementation: ‚Ä¢ Uses pre-trained Sentence-BERT model (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) ‚Ä¢ BERT embeddings for semantic understanding (Devlin et al., 2019) ‚Ä¢ Cosine similarity for angular distance measurement (Singhal, 2001) Category Confidence Score: Confidence = sigmoid(weighted_indicator_score) √ó content_quality_modifier Academic Foundation: Based on transformer architecture research (BERT), semantic similarity methods, and logistic regression principles for probability estimation.
‚úÖ Methodological Strengths:
  • Academically grounded formulas with peer-reviewed citations
  • Multi-factor scoring avoiding single-metric bias
  • Transparent weight justification using AHP principles
  • Reproducible calculations with documented parameters
  • Cross-validation through manual entry comparison
üîÑ Continuous Improvement:
  • Regular validation against expert assessment
  • Weight optimization through empirical testing
  • Source authority database updates
  • Algorithm refinement based on result quality
  • Integration of emerging NLP techniques

IMPACT CATEGORY: Determine primary category (Replace/Augment/New/Human-Only) 2. KEY FINDINGS: Extract 3-5 most important insights 3. EVIDENCE STRENGTH: Rate quality of evidence (High/Medium/Low) 4. LIMITATIONS: Identify gaps or uncertainties OUTPUT FORMAT: **Category:** [Primary impact type] **Summary:** [3-sentence executive summary] **Key Evidence:** [Bullet points of supporting evidence] **Confidence:** [High/Medium/Low] with rationale **Timeline:** [Any temporal predictions mentioned]

TREND IDENTIFICATION: What patterns emerge across articles? 2. CONSENSUS AREAS: Where do sources agree? 3. CONTRADICTIONS: Where do sources disagree? 4. EVIDENCE QUALITY: Overall strength of supporting evidence 5. GAPS: What information is missing? 6. IMPLICATIONS: Strategic implications for cybersecurity workforce OUTPUT FORMAT: **Category Overview:** [2-paragraph synthesis] **Key Trends:** [3-5 major patterns identified] **Evidence Assessment:** [Overall quality and reliability] **Confidence Level:** [High/Medium/Low for category conclusions] **Research Gaps:** [Areas needing additional investigation] **Strategic Implications:** [Workforce planning recommendations]
